C/2025 R3 PanSTARRS · April 2026

The comet's brilliant white dust tail and blue ion tail arc upward through the Milky Way core. Observers on the right ridge demonstrate the scale of this celestial event against a truly dark, high-altitude sky. The greenish coma at the nucleus is characteristic of diatomic carbon emission excited by solar ultraviolet radiation.

Conditions checklist

Do this

Go out at least 90 minutes before sunrise
Allow 20–30 min dark adaptation — red flashlight only
Find a site with a clear, flat eastern horizon
Use Great Square of Pegasus as your anchor
Look for a soft greenish smudge with a faint tail

Avoid this

Going out too late — twilight washes it out fast
Light-polluted suburban or urban skies
High humidity or mist near the horizon
Waiting past April 20 if in Northern Hemisphere
Phone screen — destroys dark adaptation
Step-by-step plan
1

Check the weather and moon phase

New moon was April 17 — currently ideal. This dark-sky window lasts only a few more days.

Tonight is excellent
2

Find your sunrise time and set an alarm

Look up local sunrise and subtract 90–100 minutes. Use the tracker above to see your local rise/set times.

3

Get to a dark location and let your eyes adapt

Drive away from streetlights. After 20 minutes your rod cells will be fully dark-adapted — you see ~10× more stars.

4

Locate the Great Square of Pegasus

Face east. Look for a large square of four bright stars 10–20° above the eastern horizon.

5

Sweep with binoculars inside the square

Move slowly in a grid pattern. Look for a fuzzy greenish patch with a faint elongated tail pointing away from the horizon.

Key step
6

Observe the ion tail

Look for the tail extending away from the Sun. On April 8 it measured over 7° long — wider than your fist at arm’s length.

7

Photograph if you can

Tripod, f/2.8–f/4, ISO 1600–3200, 10–30s. Take 20+ frames and stack in DeepSkyStacker or Siril.

Optional
Pro tips
◆

Use averted vision

Don’t look directly at the comet. Peripheral rods are far more sensitive to faint light.

◆

Dress for the cold

Pre-dawn hours are always colder than expected. Layer up and bring something warm to sit on.

◆

Forward scattering boost

After perihelion, forward scattering may dramatically boost apparent brightness but the comet will be very close to the Sun.

◆

Southern hemisphere: wait for May

Philippines, Australia, South Africa — your primary window opens in late April into May.

◆

This is a one-time event

C/2025 R3 is on a hyperbolic orbit. After this pass it will be permanently ejected from the solar system.

◆

Use a sky app

SkySafari, Stellarium, or Star Walk 2 show R3’s precise nightly position from JPL Horizons data.
